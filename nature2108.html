Telomerase Expression in ALS

by Maria Chavez

Posted on September 1, 2014 at 1:00 PM

Telomerase has been linked to several neurological disorders. Human telomerase reverse transcriptase (hTERT) expression had never been characterized in ALS. In a study plublished on August 21 on the Journal of Human Genetics, a team of researchers from Italy characterized telomerase in blood samples and spinal cord from 50 ALS patients and 50 controls.

Telomerase is an enzyme capable of reverse transcribing DNA. It is required for complete replication of telomeres. Telomerase expression is low in normal human cells, but upregulated in embryonic cells, adult male germ cells, stem cells, proliferating tissues and human malignancies. Telomerase also has a regulatory role in gene expression.

Telomeres have largely been investigated for their roles in cancer, and its dysfunction has also shown to play a role in lung disease and neurological disfunction.

In a previous study in stem cells derived from ALS patients telomerase activity was 106 fold lower than glioblastoma tumor cells, but telmoerase activity was not compared to healthy controls. Interestingly, AGS-499 increases telomerase activity and expression in spinal cord and delayed the progression of ALS in SOD1 transgenic mice and increased survival by 60%.

In post mortem tissue from sALS patients hTERT expression was 96% lower than in controls. ALS leuhocytes also showed a 5-6x decrease in mTERt RNA transcript expression compared to controls.

p53 and p21 expression was overexpressed compared to controls, and correlated to level of hTERT mRNA in ALS

The telomere length was 15% lower in sALS patients than in controls, which is probably a consequence of inactive telomerase; as telomere length was correlated to mRNA expression of hTERT in leukocytes

Summary: telomerase activity was significantly decreased in sALS spinal cord and leukocytes. It has been suggested that the role of telomerase is to support genome instability and be neuroprotective, so the lack of hTERT expression could be required for neurodegeneration. Since AGS-499 increases lifespan in SOD1 mice, it can be concluded that the lack of telomerase contributes to neuronal death in sALS.

De Felice, B., Annunziata, A., Fiorentino, G., Manfellotto, F., D’Alessandro, R., Marino, R., … Biffali, E. (2014). Telomerase expression in amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) patients. Journal of Human Genetics, (July), 1–7. doi:10.1038/jhg.2014.72
